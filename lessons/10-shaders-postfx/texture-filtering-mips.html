Textures In Depth: Filtering, Mipmaps & sRGB

Back in module 06 we got a single texture onto a cube and called it done. But the moment that cube drifts into the distance, or you tile a floor into the horizon, the naïve sampler we wrote starts to sparkle and crawl. This lesson is about sampling quality: how the GPU actually reads texels, why distant surfaces shimmer, and the three settings — filtering, mipmaps and color space — that separate a hobby renderer from one that looks calm and correct. Get these right once in MiniEngine's material system and every texture you ever load benefits.

What you'll learn
  • Why distant textures shimmer (minification) and nearby ones blur (magnification).
  • Point, bilinear, trilinear and anisotropic filtering — what each fixes and what it costs.
  • How mipmaps work, how the GPU picks a level, and how to generate the chain on D3D11 and Metal.
  • Wrap, clamp, mirror and border address modes for UVs outside 0..1.
  • The sRGB trap: why forgetting it washes your whole scene out, and the linear workflow that fixes it.

The two ways sampling goes wrong

A texture is a grid of texels. A UV coordinate is a point in that grid — (0,0) top-left, (1,1) bottom-right. The problem is that the mapping from texels to screen pixels is almost never one-to-one. Two things happen:

  • Magnification — one texel covers many screen pixels. You are up close to a low-resolution texture. Without help the result is a blocky staircase; with blending it turns soft and blurry.
  • Minification — many texels fall inside a single screen pixel. The surface is far away or at a steep angle. A point sample can only return one of those texels, and which one it hits changes as the camera moves a fraction of a pixel. That flicker is the shimmer (or aliasing / moiré) you see crawling across distant floors and fences.

Minification is the harder, more important problem, and it is exactly what mipmaps solve. But first, filtering — how the GPU turns the texels it reads into a single color.

Filtering: turning texels into a color

The filter setting on a sampler answers "given a UV, which texels do I read and how do I combine them?" There are four levels of sophistication.

Point (nearest)

Snap to the single closest texel and return it. Cheapest possible read. Up close it looks blocky — every texel is a hard square — and in the distance it shimmers badly. That blocky look is not always a bug, though: it is exactly the crisp, pixel-art aesthetic a voxel game like Minecraft wants. We will lean into that in module 11.

Bilinear

Read the four texels surrounding the UV and blend them by distance (a 2×2 weighted average). This is the everyday smooth look. It fixes magnification blockiness, but on its own it does nothing for minification shimmer — a 2×2 average still ignores the hundreds of other texels squeezed into that pixel.

Trilinear

Bilinear needs mipmaps to shine. Trilinear does a bilinear read on two mip levels — the one just larger and the one just smaller than the pixel needs — and blends between them by the fractional level. This removes the visible seam ("mip banding") where one mip level abruptly hands off to the next as a surface recedes.

Anisotropic

Bilinear and trilinear assume the pixel's footprint in texture space is a square. On a floor viewed at a grazing angle it is a long, thin ellipse — much wider in one direction than the other. A square filter has to pick a mip coarse enough to cover the long axis, which over-blurs the short axis, and your floor turns to mush toward the horizon. Anisotropic filtering takes several samples along that stretched axis, keeping grazing-angle surfaces sharp. MaxAnisotropy caps how many samples (typically 4, 8 or 16); it is the single biggest visual upgrade for terrain and floors.

A crisp voxel look without the shimmer

Point filtering and mipmaps are not enemies. For a Minecraft-style look you want texels to stay hard-edged up close but stop crawling in the distance. Keep point magnification so blocks stay sharp, but still generate mipmaps and let the minification blend between levels — in D3D11 that is D3D11_FILTER_MIN_MAG_POINT_MIP_LINEAR. Add anisotropy for long floors. We wire exactly this into the voxel renderer in module 11.

Mipmaps: pre-shrinking the image

A mipmap chain is a precomputed sequence of half-size copies of a texture: 512×512, then 256×256, 128×128, all the way down to 1×1. Each level is a box-filtered (averaged) downsample of the one above, so a distant surface can sample a level whose texels are already close to pixel-sized. Instead of one screen pixel trying to average a thousand full-res texels every frame, the GPU reads a handful from a level that was averaged once, offline.

The extra memory is modest: each level has a quarter of the pixels of the one above, so the geometric series 1/4 + 1/16 + 1/64 + … converges to ≈ 33% over the base image. That one-third overhead buys you both a huge reduction in shimmer and better texture-cache behavior, because distant draws touch small, cache-friendly levels.

How the GPU chooses a level

You never pick the mip level yourself. When a pixel shader calls Sample, the hardware runs the shader on 2×2 pixel quads and measures how fast the UV changes between neighboring pixels — the screen-space derivatives ddx(uv) and ddy(uv). A large derivative means many texels per pixel (heavy minification), so it selects a coarser (higher-numbered) level; a small derivative keeps it on a fine level. Trilinear then blends across the fractional part.

Why Sample must live in uniform flow

Because level selection relies on comparing neighboring pixels, tex.Sample(smp, uv) is only valid where all pixels in the quad take the same path — not inside a divergent if or a loop with a data-dependent length. If you must sample in a branch, compute the UV before the branch, or call SampleLevel/SampleGrad and supply the level or gradients yourself. Sampling in a dynamic loop with plain Sample gives undefined derivatives and garbage mips.

Generating the chain in Direct3D 11

You rarely author mips by hand. On Windows, create the texture with a full mip chain, the render-target bind flag, and the generate-mips misc flag; upload the full-resolution image into level 0; then let the GPU box-filter the rest with a single GenerateMips call through the SRV.

// device:  ID3D11Device*      context: ID3D11DeviceContext*
// pixels:  decoded RGBA bytes of the full-resolution image
ComPtr<ID3D11ShaderResourceView> CreateMippedTexture(
    ID3D11Device* device, ID3D11DeviceContext* context,
    const void* pixels, UINT width, UINT height)
{
    D3D11_TEXTURE2D_DESC desc = {};
    desc.Width            = width;
    desc.Height           = height;
    desc.MipLevels        = 0;   // 0 = allocate the FULL chain down to 1x1
    desc.ArraySize        = 1;
    desc.Format           = DXGI_FORMAT_R8G8B8A8_UNORM_SRGB; // see sRGB below
    desc.SampleDesc.Count = 1;
    desc.Usage            = D3D11_USAGE_DEFAULT;             // GPU can write mips
    desc.BindFlags        = D3D11_BIND_SHADER_RESOURCE |
                            D3D11_BIND_RENDER_TARGET;        // required by GenerateMips
    desc.MiscFlags        = D3D11_RESOURCE_MISC_GENERATE_MIPS;

    // No initial data here: we upload level 0 after creation, then generate.
    ComPtr<ID3D11Texture2D> tex;
    if (FAILED(device->CreateTexture2D(&desc, nullptr, &tex)))
        return nullptr;

    // Copy the full-res image into mip 0. Row pitch = width * 4 bytes (RGBA8).
    context->UpdateSubresource(tex.Get(), 0, nullptr, pixels, width * 4, 0);

    D3D11_SHADER_RESOURCE_VIEW_DESC srvDesc = {};
    srvDesc.Format                    = desc.Format;
    srvDesc.ViewDimension             = D3D11_SRV_DIMENSION_TEXTURE2D;
    srvDesc.Texture2D.MostDetailedMip = 0;
    srvDesc.Texture2D.MipLevels       = (UINT)-1; // -1 = view all levels

    ComPtr<ID3D11ShaderResourceView> srv;
    if (FAILED(device->CreateShaderResourceView(tex.Get(), &srvDesc, &srv)))
        return nullptr;

    context->GenerateMips(srv.Get()); // box-filter the whole chain from level 0
    return srv;
}

Two details make or break this: MipLevels = 0 tells Direct3D to allocate every level (setting it to 1 gives you a single level and GenerateMips becomes a no-op), and the SRV's MipLevels = -1 exposes the full chain to shaders. Production DDS pipelines instead ship pre-generated mips baked with a high-quality filter, which looks better than the GPU box filter and skips the runtime cost — but GenerateMips is perfect for textures you build or decode at load time.

Generating the chain in Metal

On macOS, ask for a mipmapped texture in the descriptor (the mipmapped: true argument sizes the chain for you), upload level 0, then let a blit encoder fill the rest with generateMipmaps:

MTL::TextureDescriptor* td =
    MTL::TextureDescriptor::texture2DDescriptor(
        MTL::PixelFormatRGBA8Unorm_sRGB, width, height, /*mipmapped*/ true);
td->setUsage(MTL::TextureUsageShaderRead);

MTL::Texture* texture = device->newTexture(td);

// Upload the full-resolution image into mip level 0.
MTL::Region region = MTL::Region::Make2D(0, 0, width, height);
texture->replaceRegion(region, /*mip*/ 0, pixels, /*bytesPerRow*/ width * 4);

// Fill levels 1..N on the GPU with a blit encoder.
MTL::CommandBuffer*      cmd  = queue->commandBuffer();
MTL::BlitCommandEncoder* blit = cmd->blitCommandEncoder();
blit->generateMipmaps(texture);
blit->endEncoding();
cmd->commit();

For higher-quality downsampling Metal also exposes Metal Performance Shaders (MPSImageGaussianPyramid and friends), but generateMipmaps is the standard, one-line path. The asset-decoding code that produces pixels stays shared C++; only these creation calls are backend-specific.

Building the sampler

Filtering, anisotropy and addressing all live on a small immutable state object — ID3D11SamplerState on Windows, MTLSamplerState on macOS. You create a handful at startup and share them across every draw. Here is the workhorse: anisotropic filtering, 8× taps, wrapping UVs.

ComPtr<ID3D11SamplerState> CreateAnisoSampler(ID3D11Device* device)
{
    D3D11_SAMPLER_DESC desc = {};
    desc.Filter         = D3D11_FILTER_ANISOTROPIC; // implies trilinear taps
    desc.MaxAnisotropy  = 8;                         // 1..16
    desc.AddressU       = D3D11_TEXTURE_ADDRESS_WRAP;
    desc.AddressV       = D3D11_TEXTURE_ADDRESS_WRAP;
    desc.AddressW       = D3D11_TEXTURE_ADDRESS_WRAP;
    desc.MipLODBias     = 0.0f;
    desc.ComparisonFunc = D3D11_COMPARISON_NEVER;
    desc.MinLOD         = 0.0f;
    desc.MaxLOD         = D3D11_FLOAT32_MAX;         // let it reach every mip

    ComPtr<ID3D11SamplerState> sampler;
    HRESULT hr = device->CreateSamplerState(&desc, &sampler);
    return SUCCEEDED(hr) ? sampler : nullptr;
}
The MaxLOD trap

Leave MaxLOD at its zero-initialized default and the sampler is clamped to mip level 0 — you generated a whole chain and the GPU never touches it, so your scene shimmers exactly as before. Always set MaxLOD = D3D11_FLOAT32_MAX (or a real cap) when you want mipmapping. It is one of those bugs that looks like "mipmaps don't work" when the mips are fine and the sampler is the problem.

The Metal equivalent decouples the three filters, so you set them independently — which, incidentally, makes the crisp-voxel combination trivial: point magFilter, mipmapped minFilter.

MTL::SamplerDescriptor* sd = MTL::SamplerDescriptor::alloc()->init();
sd->setMinFilter(MTL::SamplerMinMagFilterLinear);
sd->setMagFilter(MTL::SamplerMinMagFilterLinear);
sd->setMipFilter(MTL::SamplerMipFilterLinear);   // linear mip blend = trilinear
sd->setMaxAnisotropy(8);
sd->setSAddressMode(MTL::SamplerAddressModeRepeat); // U
sd->setTAddressMode(MTL::SamplerAddressModeRepeat); // V
MTL::SamplerState* sampler = device->newSamplerState(sd);
sd->release();

On the shader side nothing changes from module 06 — the sampler carries all of this state. The pixel shader just declares the texture and sampler and calls Sample; the hardware applies the filter, mip selection and addressing you configured.

Texture2D    tex : register(t0);
SamplerState smp : register(s0);

float4 PSMain(PSInput input) : SV_TARGET
{
    // One call. The sampler picks the mip and blends texels for us.
    float3 albedo = tex.Sample(smp, input.uv).rgb;
    // ... lighting happens here, in LINEAR space (see below) ...
    return float4(albedo, 1.0f);
}

Address modes: UVs outside 0..1

The address mode decides what a UV of, say, 1.7 means. This is how you tile a brick texture across a wall or stop a decal from smearing at its edge.

ModeD3D11 constantBehavior at UV = 1.3Good for
Wrap / RepeatTEXTURE_ADDRESS_WRAPTiles: reads 0.3Repeating brick, terrain, floors
ClampTEXTURE_ADDRESS_CLAMPSticks to the edge: reads 1.0Decals, sprites, gradients
MirrorTEXTURE_ADDRESS_MIRRORFlips each tile: reads 0.7Seamless tiling from a non-tiling image
BorderTEXTURE_ADDRESS_BORDERReturns a fixed BorderColorShadow maps, masks with a defined outside

Metal names the same four SamplerAddressModeRepeat, ClampToEdge, MirrorRepeat and ClampToBorderColor. To repeat a texture more than once across a mesh, you do not change the address mode — you scale the UVs (multiply by, say, 4.0 in the vertex shader or bake it into the mesh) and let WRAP tile the result.

The sRGB trap

This is the one that makes people ask "why does my whole scene look washed out and muddy?" — and the fix is two format changes, not a shader rewrite.

Image files that represent color — an albedo/diffuse PNG, a photo — are authored in sRGB space. Their bytes are perceptually encoded with a gamma-like curve so that the limited 0–255 range spends more precision on the darks your eye is sensitive to. A stored value of 0.5 is not half the light energy of 1.0; decoded, it is only about 0.22 of it (roughly 0.5 raised to the 2.2 power). Lighting math — multiplying by a light color, adding two contributions — only gives physically correct results in linear space, where the numbers are proportional to light energy. Multiply sRGB-encoded texels directly and every product comes out too dark and every blend comes out wrong.

You do not want to decode by hand in the shader, because filtering and mip generation must happen in linear space too. The clean fix is to let the hardware do it: create color textures with an _SRGB pixel format. Then every Sample returns already-linearized values before filtering, for free.

// COLOR textures (albedo, diffuse) — let the GPU linearize on read:
DXGI_FORMAT_R8G8B8A8_UNORM_SRGB     // D3D11
MTL::PixelFormatRGBA8Unorm_sRGB     // Metal

// The same bytes as UNORM, but sampling returns LINEAR values.
// Then, so the final image looks right, present through an sRGB
// render target / swap-chain view, which re-encodes on write:
DXGI_FORMAT_R8G8B8A8_UNORM_SRGB     // backbuffer / RTV format
Forgetting sRGB is the #1 "washed-out scene" bug

If your lit scene looks pale, flat and low-contrast, this is almost always the cause: a color texture loaded as plain UNORM and lit as if it were linear. Switch the format to _SRGB and present through an sRGB target. Because the byte layout is identical, it costs nothing at runtime and needs no shader changes — but it corrects every diffuse texture and every blend in the frame at once.

Only color is sRGB — data maps are linear

Normal, roughness, metallic, height and ambient-occlusion maps store numbers, not colors your eye judges — a normal's 0.5 really means the midpoint. Loading them as _SRGB would silently corrupt those values through the decode curve. Keep data maps as plain UNORM and reserve _SRGB for albedo and other authored-color textures. MiniEngine's material system will tag each texture slot as "color" or "data" so it can pick the right format automatically.

Wiring it into MiniEngine

Everything here lives in two places in our engine, and both are things we already have. Texture creation — the mip chain and the sRGB-vs-UNORM choice — belongs in the resource/asset loader, driven by a per-slot "is this color?" flag. Sampler creation belongs at renderer startup: build a small set (linear-wrap-aniso, point-clamp, point-wrap-mipped for voxels) once, and let each Material reference the shared sampler it wants. No per-draw sampler churn, no per-material texture format guessing. With that in place, loading a new asset is just "decode the file, pick color or data, generate mips" and the quality is correct by construction — which is exactly the setup the next lessons on atlases and render targets will build on.

Recap
  • Minification (many texels per pixel) causes shimmer; magnification (one texel over many pixels) causes blockiness or blur. Filtering and mipmaps address them.
  • Point is crisp but aliased; bilinear smooths magnification; trilinear blends mip levels; anisotropic keeps grazing-angle floors sharp. Point + mipmaps is the crisp voxel combo.
  • Mipmaps are a ~33%-larger chain of half-size downsamples; the GPU picks a level from the screen-space derivative of the UVs. Generate them with GenerateMips (D3D11) or a blit encoder's generateMipmaps (Metal), and remember MaxLOD.
  • Address modes — wrap, clamp, mirror, border — decide what UVs outside 0..1 read; tile by scaling UVs, not by changing the mode.
  • Color textures are authored in sRGB: create them as _SRGB so the GPU linearizes on read, light in linear space, and present through an sRGB target. Keep normal/roughness/data maps as linear UNORM.
  • In MiniEngine, mip + format choice lives in the asset loader; a few shared samplers live at renderer startup and are referenced by materials.
