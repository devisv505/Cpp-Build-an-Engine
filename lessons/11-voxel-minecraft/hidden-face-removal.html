Hidden Face Removal

The naive mesher from last lesson works — but it is spectacularly wasteful. A single solid 16³ chunk turns into 49,152 triangles, and almost none of them are ever seen: they are buried inside the rock, pressed flat against a neighbor. In this lesson we make one small change to the mesher that deletes those hidden faces. It is the single optimization that takes voxel rendering in our engine from "unplayable" to "runs at hundreds of frames per second," and it sets up the greedy meshing we build next.

What you'll learn
  • Why a face between two solid blocks is never visible, and how skipping it deletes 90%+ of your triangles.
  • A correct FaceVisible predicate that also handles transparency: solid|water, water|water, and water|air.
  • Why chunk edges break a naive neighbor lookup, and the seams and double walls it causes.
  • The padded 18×18×18 volume trick that folds the six neighbor chunks into one border, so the inner loop never special-cases an edge.
  • The rewritten mesher loop, and the dramatic triangle-count drop it buys.

A face between two solids is never seen

Our naive mesher walked every solid voxel and emitted all six of its faces. That is correct, but think about two stone blocks sitting side by side. The face on the right of the left block and the face on the left of the right block occupy the same plane, sandwiched between two opaque cubes. No camera can ever reach that plane — it is walled off on both sides. Drawing it is pure waste: the GPU transforms those vertices, rasterizes the triangles, and then the depth buffer throws every pixel away behind the block in front.

So the rule is simple. Emit a face only when it sits on a boundary between a block and see-through space. If the neighbor across that face is air (or water, or glass — anything you can see through), the face is on the surface and we keep it. If the neighbor is another opaque block, the face is interior and we drop it. For a chunk, this means we only ever generate its shell: the thin crust where solid meets sky, cave, or water.

Deciding whether a face is visible

Everything hinges on one predicate: given the block we are meshing (self) and the block across a face (neighbor), do we draw that face? To answer it we lean on the block registry from earlier in this module. It already hands us two properties per block, looked up straight by id from a flat table — no virtual calls, no branch on block type. 

// From the block-registry lesson: one flat table, looked up straight by id.
// BLOCK_AIR (id 0) was registered with { solid = false, transparent = true }.
inline bool IsSolid(BlockId id)       { return Blocks().Get(id).solid; }
inline bool IsTransparent(BlockId id) { return Blocks().Get(id).transparent; }

The subtlety is transparency. If you only ever had opaque stone and empty air, the rule would be "draw the face when the neighbor is air." But a voxel world has water, glass, and leaves — blocks you render and see through. That forces us to be precise about four kinds of boundary:

CurrentNeighbourDraw current's face?Why
stoneairYesA surface exposed to empty space.
stonestoneNoBuried between two opaque blocks.
stonewaterYesThe rock is still visible through the water.
waterairYesThe water's surface meets the sky.
waterwaterNoAn internal face inside a body of water — never seen.
waterstoneNoThe opaque rock hides the water face behind it.
waterglassYesTwo different see-through blocks: both surfaces show.

Every one of those rows falls out of one tiny function — the very FaceVisible helper the block-registry lesson dropped in and promised to explain here. The face is worth drawing exactly when the neighbor is see-through, with a single guard so a run of the same transparent block (a lake, a pane of glass) does not fill up with invisible internal faces. 

// A face is worth drawing only when the block across it is see-through —
// and never between two of the SAME see-through block.
inline bool FaceVisible(BlockId self, BlockId neighbor) {
    return IsTransparent(neighbor)   // air, water, glass: you can see past it
        && neighbor != self;         // ...but cull water|water and glass|glass
}
Air needs no special case

Notice FaceVisible never mentions air, and never checks that self is solid. It does not have to: the mesher loop skips air voxels with IsSolid before it ever asks about a face, so self is always a real block. And BLOCK_AIR registers as { solid = false, transparent = true }, so air as a neighbour reads as see-through — exactly what we want. That is what lets the whole predicate be one line. It is also the trap: if IsTransparent(BLOCK_AIR) ever returns false, every chunk renders as a solid cube with no visible surface at all.

The chunk-border problem

FaceVisible needs the neighbor voxel in each of the six directions. For a voxel deep inside the chunk that is trivial — the neighbor is one cell over in the same array. But look at a voxel sitting on the chunk's edge, say at local x = 0. Its neighbor in the −X direction lives at x = -1, which is not in this chunk at all: it belongs to the chunk next door.

If the mesher only knows about its own chunk, it has to guess. The tempting shortcut — "clamp to the edge, or treat anything out of range as air" — is wrong. If the neighbor chunk is solid rock, guessing "air" draws a full 16×16 wall of faces that are actually buried against that rock. You get a double wall between every pair of chunks: thousands of hidden triangles back, and a visible seam where lighting and depth fighting flicker along the border. The only correct answer is to actually read the adjacent chunk's blocks.

Forgetting cross-chunk neighbors causes seams — and stale meshes

Two bugs come from ignoring neighbor chunks. First, holes and seams: a chunk meshed as if the world ended at its edge either grows an interior wall or, worse, is missing surface faces where the neighbor is air. Second, staleness: a chunk you meshed while its neighbor was still loading was meshed against wrong (or missing) data. When a neighbor finishes loading or a block on the shared border changes, you must mark the touching chunks dirty and re-mesh them, or the border silently drifts out of sync.

A padded 18×18×18 volume

You could sprinkle the inner loop with "if this voxel is on an edge, reach into the correct neighbor chunk" checks — six directions, each with its own edge case, is a nest of bugs. There is a much cleaner trick that every serious voxel mesher uses: build the chunk into a padded volume first.

Take the 16³ chunk and copy it into the center of an 18³ array. Then fill the one-voxel border all the way around by copying the touching slab from each of the six neighbor chunks. Now the inner loop reads from this padded buffer with a single accessor that is always in bounds — even for a voxel on the edge, the neighbor cell it needs is already sitting in the border. Every edge case disappears into the setup step.

Here is the setup. We index the padded volume so a chunk-local coordinate can range over [-1, CHUNK_SIZE] — the border included — and a missing neighbor (one that has not streamed in yet) simply reads as air. 

static constexpr int CHUNK_SIZE = 16;
static constexpr int PAD        = 1;
static constexpr int PADDED     = CHUNK_SIZE + 2 * PAD;   // 18
using PaddedVolume = std::array<BlockId, PADDED * PADDED * PADDED>;

// Map a chunk-local coordinate to its slot in the padded volume.
// x, y, z may be anywhere in [-1, CHUNK_SIZE] — the 1-voxel border included.
inline int PaddedIndex(int x, int y, int z) {
    return (x + PAD) + (y + PAD) * PADDED + (z + PAD) * PADDED * PADDED;
}

// The current chunk plus the six chunks that touch its faces.
struct Neighborhood {
    const Chunk* center;
    const Chunk* west;  const Chunk* east;   // -X / +X
    const Chunk* down;  const Chunk* up;      // -Y / +Y
    const Chunk* north; const Chunk* south;   // -Z / +Z
};

void BuildPadded(const Neighborhood& n, PaddedVolume& out) {
    const int C = CHUNK_SIZE;

    // A neighbour that has not loaded yet reads as air.
    auto sample = [](const Chunk* c, int x, int y, int z) -> BlockId {
        return c ? c->GetBlock(x, y, z) : BLOCK_AIR;
    };

    // 1) Copy this chunk into the 16^3 core.
    for (int z = 0; z < C; ++z)
      for (int y = 0; y < C; ++y)
        for (int x = 0; x < C; ++x)
            out[PaddedIndex(x, y, z)] = n.center->GetBlock(x, y, z);

    // 2) Copy the one touching slab of each neighbour into the border.
    for (int a = 0; a < C; ++a)
      for (int b = 0; b < C; ++b) {
        out[PaddedIndex(-1, a, b)] = sample(n.west,  C - 1, a,     b);     // -X border
        out[PaddedIndex( C, a, b)] = sample(n.east,  0,     a,     b);     // +X border
        out[PaddedIndex(a, -1, b)] = sample(n.down,  a,     C - 1, b);     // -Y border
        out[PaddedIndex(a,  C, b)] = sample(n.up,    a,     0,     b);     // +Y border
        out[PaddedIndex(a, b, -1)] = sample(n.north, a,     b,     C - 1); // -Z border
        out[PaddedIndex(a, b,  C)] = sample(n.south, a,     b,     0);     // +Z border
      }
    // The 12 edges and 8 corners stay air; face visibility never reads them.
}
Six neighbors are enough — for now

Hidden-face removal only ever asks about the block directly across a face, so the six face-adjacent neighbors are all we need; the 12 edge and 8 corner cells of the padding can stay air. When we add ambient occlusion later in this module, each vertex will sample its diagonal neighbors too, and you will extend BuildPadded to fill those edges and corners. Same padded-volume idea, a little more copying.

Reuse one scratch buffer

A PaddedVolume is 18³ = 5,832 BlockIds, about 11 KB. Do not allocate a fresh one for every chunk you mesh — keep a single thread_local PaddedVolume scratch buffer per meshing thread and overwrite it each time. When we move meshing onto a job system later, one buffer per worker keeps the whole pass allocation-free.

The mesher, now with sight

Set it beside BuildChunkMeshNaive from last lesson and only two things change. The block data now comes from the padded buffer instead of the raw chunk, and each face is guarded by FaceVisible before we append its vertices. The neighbor direction is free — the face's own normal is the step to the neighbor — and because that coordinate lands in [-1, 16] it is always inside the padded volume, so there is no bounds check and no cross-chunk branch anywhere in the hot loop. 

#include "VoxelFaces.h"     // BlockFace kFaces[6], kCornerUV[4] — from the naive mesher
#include "BlockRegistry.h"  // IsSolid, FaceVisible, BLOCK_AIR
#include "Mesh.h"

// Same shape as BuildChunkMeshNaive, but it needs the six neighbours, so it
// meshes from the padded volume and gates every face on FaceVisible.
Mesh BuildChunkMesh(const Neighborhood& hood) {
    static thread_local PaddedVolume padded;   // one reused scratch buffer per thread
    BuildPadded(hood, padded);

    std::vector<Vertex>   vertices;
    std::vector<uint32_t> indices;

    for (int z = 0; z < CHUNK_SIZE; ++z)
    for (int y = 0; y < CHUNK_SIZE; ++y)
    for (int x = 0; x < CHUNK_SIZE; ++x) {
        BlockId here = padded[PaddedIndex(x, y, z)];
        if (!IsSolid(here))
            continue;                          // air and non-solids emit nothing

        for (int f = 0; f < 6; ++f) {
            const BlockFace& face = kFaces[f];

            // The one new line: the face normal IS the direction to the neighbour.
            BlockId neighbor = padded[PaddedIndex(
                x + int(face.normal.x),
                y + int(face.normal.y),
                z + int(face.normal.z))];
            if (!FaceVisible(here, neighbor))
                continue;                      // hidden face: skip it entirely

            // ---- identical to the naive mesher from here down ----
            const uint32_t base = static_cast<uint32_t>(vertices.size());
            for (int c = 0; c < 4; ++c) {
                Vertex v;
                v.position = { x + face.corners[c].x,
                               y + face.corners[c].y,
                               z + face.corners[c].z };
                v.normal   = face.normal;
                v.uv       = kCornerUV[c];
                vertices.push_back(v);
            }
            indices.push_back(base + 0);
            indices.push_back(base + 1);
            indices.push_back(base + 2);
            indices.push_back(base + 0);
            indices.push_back(base + 2);
            indices.push_back(base + 3);
        }
    }
    return Mesh(std::move(vertices), std::move(indices));
}

Look at what did not change: the kFaces table, the four-vertex append, the buffer-relative base offset, the winding — all byte-for-byte the naive mesher. We do not build a face any differently; the single FaceVisible gate just means we build far fewer of them. The result is still a plain std::vector<Vertex> and std::vector<uint32_t>, so Mesh::Upload and every renderer downstream are untouched.

How much did that save?

The savings are not subtle. Take a chunk that is completely full of stone. The naive mesher emits 6 faces × 2 triangles for all 4,096 voxels — 49,152 triangles. With hidden-face removal, the answer depends entirely on what surrounds it:

A full 16³ chunk…NaiveHidden-face removal
…sitting in open air49,152 tris3,072 tris (its six outer walls)
…buried, solid on all six sides49,152 tris0 tris (nothing is visible)
…a typical terrain chunktens of thousandsa few thousand (the surface crust)

A fully exposed chunk keeps only its shell: 6 sides × 16 × 16 = 1,536 quads, or 3,072 triangles — a 94% cut. A chunk deep underground, walled in by solid neighbors on every face, collapses to zero geometry: you never pay to draw rock you cannot see. Real terrain lands in between, because most chunks are mostly stone below and mostly air above, so the emitted surface is just the thin boundary where the two meet. Across a world, this single change routinely removes 90%–99% of the triangles the naive mesher would have produced — for the cost of one branch per face.

This is what makes voxels viable — greedy meshing goes further

Hidden-face removal is the optimization you cannot skip; without it a modest render distance drowns the GPU. But look back at the cross-section figure: the surviving faces still come one voxel-quad at a time, so a flat wall of stone is thousands of identical little quads. The next lesson, greedy meshing, merges those coplanar surviving faces into a handful of big rectangles — the same shell, a fraction of the vertices. Hidden-face removal decides which faces exist; greedy meshing decides how few triangles express them.

Recap
  • A face between two blocks is only visible when one side is see-through, so the mesher should emit only the boundary between a block and empty (or transparent) space — a chunk's shell, not its interior.
  • FaceVisible(self, neighbor) is one line — IsTransparent(neighbor) && neighbor != self — yielding solid|air, solid|water, and water|air while culling solid|solid and water|water.
  • Air needs no special case — it registers as not solid and transparent, so it is skipped as a source block and reads as see-through as a neighbour.
  • Voxels on a chunk edge have neighbours in adjacent chunks; guessing "air" there causes double walls and seams, so the mesher must read the real neighbour blocks.
  • Copy the chunk plus a one-voxel border from its six neighbours into an 18³ padded volume; then PaddedIndex is always in bounds and the inner loop has zero edge cases.
  • The payoff is enormous: a full chunk drops from 49,152 triangles to its ~3,072-triangle shell, or to zero when buried — usually a 90%+ cut. Re-mesh a chunk when a neighbour loads or its border changes, and greedy meshing next will merge the faces that survive.
