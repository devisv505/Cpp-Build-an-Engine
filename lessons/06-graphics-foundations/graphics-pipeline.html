The Graphics Pipeline Explained

Before we write a single line of Direct3D code, we need a mental map of what actually happens when a triangle appears on screen. The GPU is a factory, and the graphics pipeline is its assembly line: raw numbers go in one end, finished pixels come out the other. Once this picture clicks, every DirectX function we call in the coming lessons will have an obvious "home" on that assembly line — and our engine's renderer will stop feeling like magic.

What you'll learn
  • Why the GPU uses a fixed sequence of stages instead of one big function.
  • The five core Direct3D 11 stages and what each one consumes and produces.
  • How a list of vertices is turned into shaded pixels through rasterization.
  • What buffers and shaders are, at a high level, so later lessons make sense.
  • Where our engine's data (meshes, transforms, materials) will plug into the pipeline.

The GPU as an assembly line

A modern GPU renders millions of triangles every frame. It can do this because it does not treat each triangle as a one-off problem. Instead, all geometry flows through the same fixed sequence of steps, and each step is heavily optimized to do one job for thousands of items in parallel. That sequence is the graphics pipeline.

Some stages are fixed-function — the hardware does exactly one thing and you only tweak settings. Other stages are programmable: you supply a small program, called a shader, that runs once per vertex or once per pixel. Direct3D 11 exposes both kinds, and understanding which is which tells you where you get to write code and where you simply configure the machine.

Data goes in, pixels come out

The whole pipeline has one purpose: take a description of geometry (points in 3D space plus properties like color and texture coordinates) and decide what color each pixel of the final image should be. Everything else is detail.

The five core stages

Direct3D 11 has several stages, but five carry the essential story. Read them top to bottom — data flows in exactly this order, and the output of each stage becomes the input to the next.

1. Input Assembler (IA)

The pipeline starts here. The Input Assembler reads your raw vertex data out of a vertex buffer and, optionally, an index buffer, then groups those vertices into primitives — usually triangles. You also tell it the topology (for example, "treat every three vertices as one triangle"). Its output is a stream of assembled primitives ready to be transformed.

2. Vertex Shader (VS)

This is the first place you write a program. The Vertex Shader runs once per vertex. Its main job is to take a vertex's position in model space and transform it — through the world, view, and projection matrices — into clip space, the coordinate system the GPU needs to figure out where the vertex lands on screen. It can also pass along extra data (colors, normals, texture coordinates) for later stages. One vertex in, one transformed vertex out.

This is where our Transform lives

When our engine gives an object a Transform component (position, rotation, scale), that data ends up as matrices fed to the Vertex Shader. Moving a cube in the world is really just handing the VS a different world matrix each frame.

3. Rasterizer

The Rasterizer is fixed-function and does the crucial hand-off from geometry to image. It takes each triangle (now in screen space), figures out exactly which pixels the triangle covers, and generates a fragment for every one of them. Along the way it interpolates the per-vertex data across the triangle's surface, so a fragment in the middle of a triangle gets a smooth blend of its three corners' colors and coordinates. This is the moment vertices become pixels. It also clips geometry outside the screen and can cull back-facing triangles to save work.

4. Pixel Shader (PS)

The second programmable stage. The Pixel Shader runs once per fragment and decides its final color. This is where lighting, texture sampling, and material logic happen — the shading that makes a flat triangle look like brick, metal, or skin. One fragment in, one color out.

5. Output Merger (OM)

The final stage blends each shaded fragment into the render target (the image you will present to the window). It runs the depth test so nearer objects correctly hide farther ones, applies any transparency blending, and writes the result. After the Output Merger, your pixel is done.

There are more stages than these five

Between the Vertex Shader and Rasterizer, Direct3D 11 also offers the Hull, Tessellator, and Domain shaders (for tessellation) and the Geometry Shader (for creating or discarding primitives). They are optional and powerful, but we will leave them disabled for a long time. Ignore them for now — the five core stages are all our engine needs to draw its first mesh.

Following one triangle through

Let's trace a single triangle to make the flow concrete. Suppose we want to draw one red triangle:

  1. IA: reads three vertices from the vertex buffer and assembles them into one triangle primitive.
  2. VS: runs three times (once per vertex), transforming each corner into clip space.
  3. Rasterizer: discovers the triangle covers, say, 4,000 pixels and emits 4,000 fragments, interpolating position and color across them.
  4. PS: runs 4,000 times, returning red (or a lit variation) for each fragment.
  5. OM: depth-tests each fragment and writes the survivors into the render target.

Notice how the work multiplies: three vertices ballooned into thousands of pixels. That is exactly why the GPU is built for massive parallelism, and why we care about feeding it efficiently.

Buffers and shaders: the two things you supply

To make the pipeline run, you provide two categories of resource. We will build both in the next lessons, so here is just enough vocabulary to recognize them.

You supplyWhat it isPipeline stage it feeds
Vertex bufferAn array of vertices (positions, colors, UVs, normals)Input Assembler
Index bufferA list of indices that reuse vertices to form trianglesInput Assembler
Constant bufferSmall, per-draw data such as transform matricesVertex & Pixel Shaders
Vertex shaderA small HLSL program run per vertexVertex Shader stage
Pixel shaderA small HLSL program run per fragmentPixel Shader stage
TexturesImages sampled to color surfacesPixel Shader (usually)

Shaders are written in HLSL (High-Level Shading Language), a C-like language that runs on the GPU. Here is the shape of a minimal vertex/pixel shader pair so the words above have a face. Do not worry about the syntax yet — just see that each is a tiny function with a clear input and output, matching the stages we described. 

// A constant buffer: per-draw data uploaded from C++ (our Transform ends up here)
cbuffer PerObject : register(b0)
{
    float4x4 worldViewProj; // combined transform matrix
};

// What the Input Assembler hands to the Vertex Shader
struct VSInput
{
    float3 position : POSITION;
    float4 color    : COLOR;
};

// What the Vertex Shader passes down to the Pixel Shader
struct VSOutput
{
    float4 position : SV_POSITION; // clip-space position (required)
    float4 color    : COLOR;
};

// Vertex Shader: runs once per vertex
VSOutput VSMain(VSInput input)
{
    VSOutput output;
    // Transform the model-space point into clip space
    output.position = mul(float4(input.position, 1.0f), worldViewProj);
    output.color    = input.color; // pass color through for interpolation
    return output;
}

// Pixel Shader: runs once per fragment, returns the final color
float4 PSMain(VSOutput input) : SV_TARGET
{
    return input.color; // the rasterizer already blended the corner colors
}
SV_POSITION is not optional

The vertex shader must output a clip-space position tagged with the SV_POSITION semantic. Without it the Rasterizer has no idea where the triangle belongs on screen, and you will get nothing but a blank window — one of the most common first-triangle bugs.

Why this matters for our engine

Every renderer, including the ones inside Unity and Unreal, ultimately drives this same pipeline. When our engine grows a MeshRenderer component, its job will be to bind a vertex buffer, upload the object's transform into a constant buffer, set the right shaders, and issue a draw call. The pipeline you just met is the target of all that plumbing. Keeping this five-stage picture in your head will make the upcoming, more detailed lessons feel like filling in a diagram you already understand rather than memorizing disconnected API calls.

macOS — the same pipeline in Metal

Metal exposes the same programmable pipeline with different object names. The engine-level stages do not change:

Direct3D commands go through a device context. Metal records commands through an MTL::RenderCommandEncoder inside a command buffer. Our renderer interface names the intent—bind pipeline, bind mesh, draw—so gameplay never depends on either vocabulary.

Recap
  • The graphics pipeline is a fixed sequence of stages that turns vertices into pixels.
  • The five core stages are Input Assembler, Vertex Shader, Rasterizer, Pixel Shader, and Output Merger.
  • The Vertex Shader and Pixel Shader are programmable (you write HLSL); the others you configure.
  • The Rasterizer is where geometry becomes fragments and per-vertex data gets interpolated.
  • You feed the pipeline with buffers (vertex, index, constant) and shaders — the resources we build next.
  • Optional tessellation and geometry stages exist, but we leave them off until much later.
